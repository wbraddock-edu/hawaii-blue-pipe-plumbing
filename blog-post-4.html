Common Plumbing Issues in Older Hawaii Homes

May 24, 2024 · Blue Pipe Plumbing Hawaii

Hawaii's housing stock is unique in the US. The state has a high proportion of homes built between the 1940s and 1970s, many of them using materials and techniques common in that era but now known to present long-term problems. If you own or are considering buying an older home in Hawaii, knowing what to look for can save you from expensive surprises.

Galvanized Steel Pipe

Homes built before 1970 often have galvanized steel water supply pipes. These pipes have an expected lifespan of 40-70 years, meaning many are at or past their service life. As galvanized pipes corrode from the inside, they develop scale buildup, reduced water pressure, brown or rusty water, and eventually leaks. Re-piping with copper or PEX is the correct solution — partial repairs typically just delay the inevitable.

Cast Iron Drain Lines

Cast iron drain pipes were standard through the 1960s and have a good long-term record, but they're not permanent. After 50-60 years, cast iron can develop extensive corrosion, cracks, and root infiltration. A camera inspection of main drain and sewer lines is one of the most valuable inspections you can order for an older home — the cost is usually $150-200 and can reveal serious issues before they become emergency situations.

Lead Solder in Copper Joints

Copper pipe became standard in Hawaii homes from the 1960s onward, but early copper installations used lead-based solder at joints. Lead solder was banned in the US in 1986, but millions of homes still have it. For homes built between 1960 and 1986, a water quality test is advisable. We can help identify whether lead solder is present and advise on filtration or replacement options.

If you're buying an older Hawaii home, we offer pre-purchase plumbing inspections that document the current state of all systems and provide repair cost estimates. This information can be valuable in purchase price negotiations and helps you plan for future maintenance costs.
